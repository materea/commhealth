    Introduction

    • Monkeypox is a viral zoonosis and a disease of public health importance.
    • Monkeypox primarily occurs in central and west Africa, often in the proximity to tropical rainforest and has been increasingly appearing in urban areas.
    • Monkeypox is caused by monkeypox virus which is an enveloped double stranded DNA virus that belongs to the orthopoxvirus genus of the poxiviridae family.
    • They are two distinct genetic clades of monkeypox virus; the central Africa clade and the West Africa clade.
    • Various species has been identified with monkeypox virus and this include the rope squirrels, tree squirrels, Gambian rats, dormice, non-human primates and other species.

    Epidemiology

    • Human monkeypox was first identified in humans in 1970 in the republic of Congo
    • Since 1970 human monkeypox has been identified in 11 Africa countries which include Benin, Cameroon, Gabon, Congo DR, Cote d‚Äôivoire, Liberia, sierra Leon, Nigeria, Central Republic of Africa, South Sudan
    • In 2017, Nigeria has experience a large outbreak with over 500 suspected cases and 200 confirmed cases and a case fatality ratio of about 3%
    • Monkeypox is a disease of global public health importance
    • Not only affects countries in west and east Africa but the rest of the world.
    • In 2003, there was monkeypox outbreak in the United States of America
    • This led to an outbreak of 70 cases in U.S
    • In May 2022, multiple cases of monkeypox were identified in several non-endemic countries
    • Monkeypox infection has no racial predilection
    • It is said to be more common in males than females
    • It is commoner among homosexuals (men who has sex with men), white and H.I.V positive patient
    • Greater number of cases are found among persons younger than 15yrs

    Transmission

    • The virus can be transmitted from direct contact which include:
      • Animal to human
      • Human to human
      • Contaminated environment to human
      • Fetomaternal transmission
    • It is still unclear if monkeypox can be sexually transmitted
    • Animal to human transmission can occur through direct contact with blood, body fluids or cutaneous or mucosal lesion of infected animal.
    • Monkeypox has been found in animals like Gambian rats, squirrels, dormice and different species of monkey.
    • Natural reservoir is not yet identified
    • Eating inadequately cooked meat or bush meat of infected animals is a risk factor
    • People living in forested areas are likely to be expose to infected animals
    • Human to human transmission results from close contact with respiratory secretions, skin lesions or respiratory droplets of an infected person
    • Health workers and close household members are at greater risk
    • Indirect contact through contaminated cloths and linens.
    • Transmission can also occur via the placental from mother to fetus or during close contact during and after birth.

    Signs and symptoms

    • The incubation period of monkeypox is usually from 6 to 13 days but can range from 5 to 21 days.
    • The infection can be divided into two phases: the pre eruptive and eruptive phase.
    • The pre eruptive phase lasts about 0-5 days characterized by fever, intense headache, lymphadenopathy, myalgia, back pain, and intense asthenia
    • Lymphadenopathy is a distinctive feature of monkeypox compared to other diseases that appears similar
    • The eruptive phase usually begins within 1-3 days of appearance of fever
    • The rash tends to be more concentrated on the face and extremities rather than on the trunk
    • The rash evolves sequentially from macules to papules, then from vesicles, pustules and crusts which usually dry up and fall off.
    • The number of lesions varies from few to several thousands.
    • In severe cases lesion can coalesce until large sections of skin slough off.

    Diagnosis

    • A definitive diagnosis can be considered if appropriate clinical case definition has been met and the diagnosis has been confirmed by laboratory testing.
    • Confirmation of monkeypox depends on type and quality of the specimen and the type of laboratory test.
    • Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is the preferred laboratory test.
    • The diagnostic samples for monkeypox are from skin lesions
    • The fluid from vesicles, pustules and dry crust are collected and stored in a dry sterile bottle and should be kept cold

    Management

    • The disease is usually self-limiting, resolution occurs in 2-4 weeks
    • Patient often feel poorly during the febrile stage of the illness therefore bed rest along with supportive care are necessary
    • Use of analgesic and adequate fluid intake might be necessary
    • Severe cases often require hospitalization and airborne and contact precautions should be applied by the healthcare workers
    • Isolation should be continued until the last crust is shed
    • Antiviral agent like the tecovirimat has also be found useful in the management

    Prevention

    • Health education
    • Avoid contact with animals that could harbor the virus
    • Avoid contact with sick humans or animals
    • Isolate infected patients from others
    • Practice good hand hygiene and other standard precautions
    • Use of personal protective equipment when caring for patients
    • Thorough cooking of all animal products before eating
    • Surveillance and epidemiological studies

    Vaccination

    • Vaccination against smallpox was demonstrated through several observational studies to be 85% effective in preventing monkeypox
    • A newer vaccine based on a modified vaccine virus was approved for the prevention of monkeypox in 2019.
    • This is a two dose vaccine for which availability remains limited
    • Vaccine is not available in Nigeria

    Complications

    • Secondary bacterial infection
    • Bronchopneumonia
    • Respiratory distress
    • Keratitis
    • Corneal ulceration
    • Septicemia
    • Encephalitis

    Differential diagnosis

    • Small pox
    • Chicken pox
    • Measles
    • Allergic skin reaction
    • Bacterial skin infections
    • Scabies

    Prognosis

    • Mortality rates ranging from 1-10% has been reported in Africa
    • No fatalities occurred in United States
    • Death rates are high in Africa children
    • Other factors influencing prognosis include:
    • Health status
    • Comorbidities
    • Vaccination status
    • Severity of complication.

    Challenges to monkeypox control

    • Lack of surveillance in animals especially wild animals
    • Poor knowledge of the disease
    • Lack of knowledge of true burden of the disease
    • Inadequate resources for case based surveillance
    • Inadequate diagnostics capacities
    • Vaccination
    • Trans boundary animal movement policy

    Summary

    • Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by monkeypox virus
    • Symptoms include headache, fever, lymphnode enlargement and rash
    • It is usually self-limiting
    • It is transmitted to humans through close contact with infected animal or persons.
    • Preventive measures include avoiding contact with infected animals and person and frequent hand washing
    • Vaccine used during smallpox also provide protection against monkeypox
